Background

Origins

Before I was a product designer, I made board games that provoked, entertained, and occasionally got seized by police.

TerrorBull Games taught me everything I still use: how to communicate complex ideas through systems, how play disarms authority, and how the most effective way to challenge assumptions is to make people experience them.

What happened

War on Terror: The Boardgame

In 2006, after two years of paper prototyping, TerrorBull Games released War on Terror: The Boardgame. It was deliberately kitsch — cheap plastic components, garish colours, and a balaclava included in every box. The aesthetic was a provocation: if you're going to make a game about geopolitical conflict, don't pretend it's elegant.

It became a cultural phenomenon. International press coverage. Exhibition at the Berlin Academy of Arts. An appearance on The IT Crowd. Police seizures in multiple countries. Debates about whether satire crosses into incitement. For two years, a board game about terrorism was front-page news.

Why it matters

Play as a way of knowing

Board games are systems made tangible. Every rule is a hypothesis about human behaviour. Every mechanic is an argument. When you play War on Terror, you're not reading about asymmetric conflict — you're experiencing the frustration of impossible choices, the temptation of disproportionate response, the gap between stated values and emergent behaviour.

This is the same thing I do in product design, just with different materials. A workshop game, a feed architecture, an executive pitch — each is a system designed to produce a specific kind of understanding in the people who interact with it.

Play disarms authority. It creates a space where people can explore dangerous ideas without dangerous consequences.

Beyond TerrorBull

A broader practice of provocation and communication

TerrorBull was not a one-off. The studio produced memory games about forgetfulness, satirical games about the financial crisis, and experiments in what games could say that other media couldn't. Alongside the games:

  • Public speaking — Nudgestock talks on the persuasive power of play
  • Teaching — guest lectures at Goldsmiths, including a choose-your-own-adventure format
  • Exhibition — Now Play This festival artist presentations
  • Writing — artist texts and talk notes on play, persuasion, and systems

What it proves

The through-line

Everything since TerrorBull has been a variation on the same question: how do you help people understand something complex well enough to act on it?

At Antare, the complexity is AI-generated incident feeds. At Citrix, it was organisational wellbeing. At VMware, it was a commercial conversion journey. In workshops, it's whatever assumptions a team hasn't examined yet.

The materials change. The capability doesn't. I make systems that help people see clearly — whether those systems are board games, product architectures, research frameworks, or workshop activities.

Connection to today

My approach to product design was shaped before I entered the field. TerrorBull taught me that the most powerful communication happens when people experience an idea rather than being told about it — a principle that runs through every case study in this portfolio.

Andrew Sheerin — Product Design Leader
