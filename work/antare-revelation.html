Case study · Antare

Designing the Psychology of Attention

The feed worked perfectly. It summarised incidents accurately. Users trusted the AI. And yet everyone kept saying the same thing: "It's overwhelming."

Role
Head of Product Design
Organisation
Antare
Focus
Editorial responsibility in AI-generated intelligence
Disciplines
Product strategy, behavioural insight, IA, AI evaluation
I

Act 1

We thought we were designing an event feed

Antare set out to disrupt physical security with an always-on body-worn camera — a massive decrease in missed events, and a massive increase in observable reality. Cameras capture continuously. Models classify. Incidents appear. The obvious product move was somewhere to surface it all.

Competitors' digital evidence systems looked like datagrids: rows of clips, sortable columns, filter panels. I designed a top-of-the-range version of the same thing. It was capable. It was familiar. It was wrong for what our hardware would actually produce.

If the cameras worked, no one would parse this in a table. We needed to compress reality — not display all of it.

II

Act 2

Making AI understandable

We looked to social feeds — LinkedIn, Facebook — as adjacent experience models. If operational workflows could borrow feed behaviour, we might have a product core to match the intelligence of the hardware.

But feeds don't work without contracts. With our AI engineer, I co-defined what an incident is: identification rules, impact severity, event type, narrative structure, and a validation checklist so LLM output renders directly into UI — field by field — without a translation layer.

Every AI output field mapped to a feed affordance. Impact colour. Scannable title. One-line summary. Thumbnail and key moments threaded through to evidence. If a field didn't earn a place on the card, it didn't belong in the feed. Cross-validation between layers meant that even a wrongly classified event didn't destroy trust — internal consistency made the fix self-evident.

Research with prospective customers revealed two mental models: research & review (open-ended, information gathering) and respond (single-minded, fire-fighting). We split the feed into two modes — chronological breadth for awareness, priority ordering for action. Same underlying incidents. Different jobs to be done.

Reader thinks: nice. Clever. Most portfolios would stop here.

III

Act 3

Reality arrives

Everything looked right until we got our first full day of real footage.

Up to that point we'd tested with synthetic data, actor-generated video, office CCTV. It all looked "right." Then we partnered with prospective customers and turned always-on cameras on a live shop floor.

At that point it got really messy. The feed worked — technically. Summaries were accurate. Classification held. Users trusted the AI. And yet the word we heard constantly was overwhelming.

Not because AI failed. Not because summaries were bad. Because something felt wrong about what was appearing — and what it seemed to demand of people looking at it.

We assumed we had an AI problem. Then a prioritisation problem. Then a threshold problem. Still no.

IV

Act 4

The investigation

Instead of arguing in the abstract, I classified what "wrong" actually meant. Three types — a taxonomy the team could point at rather than debate around.

Wrong type 1

Factually incorrect. The model misread what happened — faulty product handling flagged as misconduct, context missed. Easy to catch. We built auto-feedback pipelines to route these into LLM correction funnels.

Wrong type 2

Factually correct, never useful. Employee wellbeing matters — but a feed card is not how you uncover or address it. Some truths don't belong in an operational surface at all.

Wrong type 3

Factually correct, not individually useful. Security and efficiency patterns that only make sense in aggregate — parcel-process friction recurring across a day, not as twelve separate incidents. Wait… why is this here?

Type three caused the heated arguments. Some wanted to raise the threshold score. Others wanted a second LLM to decide what to show. Studying each event — and which type of wrong it was — led somewhere deeper than either fix.

There was something in the very appearance of an event on a card in a feed that implied duty to act. Every card asked: why am I seeing this? and what do I do?

V

Act 5

Visibility creates obligation

Feeds are not neutral. Every item says: look at me. Which immediately creates: should I do something?

I coined the phrase to name what we were seeing — not a UX heuristic, but a hidden contract. Visibility alone creates obligation. Once we had that rule, we viewed the entire console through an editorial lens: why is this being shown? Why here? Why now? What do we expect people to do about it?

Everything else fell into place. The feed shrank to individually salient events that can — and should — be investigated in isolation. Everything else moved to surfaces built for how intelligence actually needs to appear:

  • Feed Operational, individual, actionable now — editorial judgement about what deserves immediate human attention.
  • Dashboard Patterns, aggregation, repetition — intelligence that only makes sense across time and volume.
  • Daily & weekly summaries Trends, emotional truth, friction points and successes — human stories, not incident rows.

We stopped thinking about where information could be shown and started asking where it deserved to exist. Every surface carries an implicit promise: feeds promise immediacy; dashboards promise patterns; summaries promise reflection. Those promises became more important than the data itself.

What it proves

This is not really a story about feeds. It is a story about editorial responsibility — designing the psychology of attention. Newspapers, social media, dashboards, security software, AI assistants: the domain changes; the question doesn't.

I went into Antare believing the challenge would be helping people trust AI-generated events. I left believing the harder problem is deciding what deserves a person's attention in the first place.

Compressing reality is complex and multi-layered; each omission is a decision that needs understanding and justifying for an automated system to be trusted.
