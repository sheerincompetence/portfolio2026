The operational feed trap: when AI visibility outruns human attention

Antare's body-worn camera platform proved that accurate AI summaries are not enough. The harder design problem is deciding what deserves a person's time.

Andrew Sheerin · Case study

01 · Trust, then volume

The feed worked. It summarised incidents accurately and users trusted the AI. The failure mode arrived with scale: five clips per camera per day, two hundred always-on devices, every day.

02 · Industry defaults

Digital evidence management systems converge on datagrids: sortable rows, dense metadata, filter panels. Antare built a competitive datagrid and still found it the wrong interface genre for continuous monitoring.

Industry DEMS

Antare datagrid

The strategic pivot: from spreadsheet semantics to feed semantics — a familiar social rhythm applied to high-stakes operational video.

03 · Contracts and dual modes

Event cards encode AI output as legible contracts: severity, category, confidence. Each field implies an action. The same organisation runs two feeds — audit chronology and priority triage — against identical underlying data.

Synthetic validation — actors, office CCTV — produced a polished experience. Production data broke the assumption.

04 · Taxonomy of wrongness

User research surfaced three failure modes: false positives, true-but-trivial events, and true-and-important events lost in volume. Fixing the feed required unpicking which kind of "wrong" each card represented.

Visibility creates obligation.

05 · Product architecture shift

Resolution: shrink the feed's scope. Expand dashboards for pattern detection and daily summaries for orientation. The feed becomes an index, not an archive.
