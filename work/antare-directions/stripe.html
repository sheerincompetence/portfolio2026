Antare

When the feed worked, and why that wasn't enough

The AI summarised incidents accurately. Users trusted it. And yet. Something was wrong — not with the technology, but with what we were asking people to look at.

I · The scale

Five clips per camera, per day

Press record on a body-worn camera and the math changes overnight. Two hundred always-on feeds. Every day. The volume alone would have broken any interface designed for occasional review.

II · The wrong tool

Everyone builds a datagrid

Digital evidence management systems default to tables. Rows of events, sortable columns, filters. We built the best datagrid we could — and it still felt like the wrong genre for the job.

The pivot was not incremental. We moved from spreadsheet to feed.

III · Making AI legible

Every field is a contract

Event cards are not decoration. Each label — severity, category, confidence — is a promise about what the model saw and what the human should do next. Classification UI is trust infrastructure.

Two feeds for the same organisation on the same day: chronological for audit, priority for triage. Same data, different obligation.

With synthetic data — actors, office CCTV — it looked great. The design held. Then we turned the cameras on.

Real data

"It's overwhelming."

IV · The investigation

Three types of wrong

Not every card deserved attention. Some were false positives. Some were true but trivial. Some were true and important but arrived in a flood that made action impossible. We had to unpick wrongness before we could fix the feed.

Visibility creates obligation.

V · Resolution

The feed shrinks; the product expands

We stopped trying to show everything. Dashboards for patterns. Summaries for the day. The feed became a pointer, not a warehouse.

I went in believing the challenge was trust. I left believing the harder problem is deciding what deserves a person's attention in the first place. Design for AI is not only about making the model legible — it is about protecting human attention from the model's enthusiasm.
