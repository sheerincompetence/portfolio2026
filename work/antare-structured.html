Case study · Antare

Compressing Reality

When AI generates more signal than anyone can process, the design problem becomes editorial — deciding what deserves attention, and for whom.

Role
Head of Product Design
Organisation
Antare
Focus
AI incident feeds, body-worn camera intelligence
Disciplines
Product strategy, IA, AI UX evaluation, behavioural insight
¶

Prologue

Always-on body cameras fundamentally change surveillance. Traditional security systems miss incidents, but ours risked creating too many.

The challenge appeared obvious: how to compress reality into something a human can understand.

Act 1

2

New methods require new models

Antare set out to disrupt physical security by introducing an intelligent body-worn camera that is always on — massively increasing observable reality. We needed a place and a method to surface it.

3

The datagrid trap

The traditional datagrid that forms the backbone of every DEMS (Digital Evidence Management System) works great when humans have pre-filtered your event list by pressing "record."

But a data grid clearly wasn't going to work when the number of clips goes from 5 per camera, per day to 200+.

4

Borrowing from somewhere unexpected

Social media had already solved one problem: humans scanning large numbers of events — not operationally, but cognitively. Maybe this could work for us…

Reimagining the data-table as a foundational admin tool following the workshop: The box-and-arrows flow I did just a couple of weeks back feels outdated and just wrong for something potentially so sophisticated and high tech. Realising the data table is just a tool to get the user to a goal has helped sidestep it — or even imagine what other tools might work as well. The result is an idea that our interface might have much more in common with a social media feed than an excel spreadsheet.

Act 2 · Making AI understandable

5

Contracts between AI and human

Populating the feed are countless AI-identified and AI-summarised events. Even camera-wearers pressing their button have the resulting footage parsed by AI.

But to do that right, we need to generate: narrative, identity, trust, classification and explanation. We needed frameworks or contracts between AI and human that would answer:

  • What constitutes an event?
  • What sort of event is it?
  • What is the overall story and main narrative beats?
  • Why might it be important?
6

Anatomy of an event card

Event cards are the atoms of the feed and need to answer all of the above. They are comprised of inter-connected chunks of system and AI-generated data that can be independently and collectively verified:

Layer Source Design job
Human action row Other users in the system Update, reassure, validate
Status row (date, button press, new-for-you, incident ID) System + wearer Urgency + orientation
Title AI Scannable in 2 seconds
Impact colour lozenge AI + design system "How worried should I be?"
Summary (short) AI One-line story for the feed
Thumbnail + key moments AI + video UX Trust / verification
Metadata (length, camera name, wearer name) System Context, geographic and staff orientation

Impact level, title language, key moment, thumbnail choice and ultimately video and transcript evidence all line up and agree with each other. Even an event that is wrongly classified or overstated doesn't erode user trust because there is still visible internal consistency.

7

We realised there wasn't one feed, but two

Interviewing security professionals and potential customers, it became clear that there were two distinct working and mental models:

  • Research & review — open minded, slow at times, information gathering, not necessarily guided by a single goal
  • Respond — focused, single-minded, guided by a single goal, fire-fighting, fixing and minimising

Chronological ("all events") — broader impact mix, sorted by time; includes not just operationally risky but lower-signal neutral/positive events by default. Chronological order is naturally stochastic and so perfect for research and review modes.

Priority — concern + risk + critical only, sorted by importance.

8

Everything looked right

We tested the feed with: customers, dummy data, synthetic data, actors improvising events for us, footage of us in the office.

It looked great. Worked better than we expected.

Then…

Act 3 · Reality arrives

9

Then we received our first full day of footage from a real customer

And it was totally wrong.

Not because AI failed. Not because summaries were bad. Because something felt wrong.

The customer summed it up best:

"It's overwhelming."

Act 4 · The investigation

10

For something so clearly broken, there was pressure to immediately fix it

But the interesting thing wasn't that real data felt "wrong" in the feed — it was that it was wrong in several different ways. As I unpacked the feed, I identified 3 types of wrong:

  • Factually incorrect = training problem; easy to fix
  • Factually correct but never useful = threshold problem; easy to fix
  • Factually correct but not individually useful = presentation problem; harder to fix

Act 5 · The hidden contract

11

There was something in the very appearance of an event that implied some sort of expectation to act.

Visibility creates obligation

Every card says: "Look at me; there's a reason I exist" — which immediately creates the question "Should I do something?"

That's the hidden contract.

12

Once we saw it, everything changed

Feeds are not neutral, they are editorial. But nothing is neutral within an operational space.

We weren't designing an operational intelligence platform… we were designing a collection of obligations.

13

The feed shrinks; the product expands

"Visibility creates obligation" gave us a strategy for building out different intelligence surfaces for different kinds of information:

  • The Feed — Operational, individual, can be dealt with immediately
  • Data Dashboard — Patterns, data aggregation, repetition, analysis across time
  • Daily/Weekly Summaries — Trends, emotional truth, outcomes, friction points and successes
14

Where information deserves to exist

We stopped thinking about where information could be shown and started asking where it deserved to exist.

Every surface carries an implicit promise:

  • Feeds promise immediacy.
  • Dashboards promise patterns.
  • Summaries promise reflection.

Those promises became vital framings for understanding the data itself.

15

Beyond the product

I see this heuristic everywhere I look now. Regardless of AI, it's a nice reminder that good design needs to come pre-armed with answers for the following:

  • Does this belong here?
  • Why now?
  • Why this person?
  • What expectation does this create?
  • What promise does this surface make?

Reflections

We assumed we had an AI problem. Then we assumed we had a prioritisation problem. Then we assumed we had a categorisation problem. But the issue ran much deeper.

This isn't a story about feeds or AI or event categorisation — it's a story about designing around the psychology of attention itself.

I went into Antare believing the challenge would be helping people trust AI-generated events. But that was easy compared to deciding and decoding what deserves a person's attention in the first place.
