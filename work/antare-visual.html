I · The wrong container

New methods need new models

The setup

Always-on cameras change the math

Antare's body-worn cameras don't wait for someone to press record. Observable reality increases by orders of magnitude — and with it, the obligation to compress it into something a human can act on.

The datagrid trap

The data table is just a tool to get the user to a goal. Our interface might have much more in common with a social media feed than an excel spreadsheet.

Design diary · pivot moment

II · Making AI legible

Every field is a contract

Before the feed could work

We co-defined what an incident is — with our AI engineer, field by field — so model output renders directly into UI without a translation layer.

What constitutes an event? What type is it? What's the story? Why might it matter? Classification frameworks answered each question before a single card appeared.

Anatomy of an event card

If it doesn't earn a place on the card, it doesn't belong in the feed

Each layer can be verified independently. Together they build trust — even when the AI is wrong.

  • Title Scannable in two seconds
  • Impact How worried should I be?
  • Summary One-line story for the feed
  • Evidence Thumbnail, key moments, transcript
  • Context Camera, wearer, geography

Two modes of attention

Same org, same day — different job to be done

Security professionals context-switch between browsing for patterns and fighting fires. One feed couldn't serve both. We built two baked-in modes on the same incident contract.

We tested with synthetic data, actors, office CCTV. It looked great. Worked better than we expected.

III · Reality arrives

Everything looked right until it didn't

Then we got our first full day on the shop floor

Not wrong because AI failed. Not wrong because summaries were bad. Wrong because of what appeared — and what each card seemed to demand.

"It's overwhelming."

Customer, first full day of live footage

IV · The investigation

Three types of wrong

Type 1

Factually incorrect. The model misread reality. A training problem — painful, but fixable with feedback loops.

Easy to fix

Type 2

Correct, never useful. True observations that don't belong in anyone's queue. A threshold problem.

Easy to fix

Type 3

Correct, not individually useful. Patterns that only make sense in aggregate — surfaced one card at a time. A presentation problem. This one started the real argument.

Harder to fix — and the interesting one

V · The hidden contract

What every card implies

There was something in the very appearance of an event that implied duty to act — not failure of the model, but failure of the surface.

Visibility creates obligation

Every card says look at me — which immediately asks should I do something? We weren't building an intelligence platform. We were designing a collection of obligations.

The feed shrinks; the product expands

Where information deserves to exist

We stopped asking where data could be shown and started asking where it deserved to be shown.

The feed

Immediacy

Individual, operational, actionable now. Editorial judgement about what earns a person's attention in this moment.

Dashboard

The feed after editorial discipline

What it proves

We assumed an AI problem. Then a prioritisation problem. Then a categorisation problem. The issue ran deeper — into the psychology of attention itself.

I went into Antare believing the challenge was helping people trust AI-generated events. I left believing the harder problem is deciding what deserves a person's attention in the first place.
