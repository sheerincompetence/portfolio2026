Case file AS-001 · Antare

When AI generates more signal than anyone can process, the design problem becomes editorial — deciding what deserves attention, and for whom.

§1 Context

A camera sees everything. The feed must not.

Antare builds intelligence from body-worn camera footage — turning continuous video into structured events that operators can act on. The AI pipeline works: cameras capture, models classify, incidents appear.

Early feeds were clogged with neutral and low-value events. Routine customer service flagged as incidents. Colleagues gossiping classified as "inappropriate conduct." Break-room behaviour judged as on-floor misconduct.

§2 Challenge

From datagrid to editorial system

The first design iteration — a sophisticated datagrid — treated the feed as a database to be sorted and filtered. Operators don't need more columns. They need a system that understands why someone is looking, and what kind of attention each event deserves.

Three layers of complexity had to be separated: Reality (immutable media), Narrative (AI-generated incidents), and Evidence (assembled cases for action).

§3 Approach

Two feeds for two modes of attention

Operators context-switch between passive awareness and active triage. These require different information architectures, ranking logic, and interaction patterns.

Main Feed: narrative/awareness, mixed valence, probabilistic ranking, per-user. Designed for browsing.

Triage Feed: urgency/action, negative-only, deterministic, shared. Designed for processing.

§4 Evidence

Systematic audit of AI-generated events

36 events reviewed and scored on truthfulness, usefulness, impact categorisation, and review queue assignment. Key patterns identified:

  • Over-generation: routine interactions flagged as notable
  • Exaggeration: LLM invents severity for normal behaviour
  • Context blindness: location misinterpreted
  • Aggregate value missed at individual event level
§5 Findings

Primary finding

AI product design is not primarily a UI problem. It is an editorial problem: deciding what deserves to exist in someone's attention at all.

The most valuable design work was the conceptual model, the evaluation framework, and the decision to split one feed into two when use cases demanded it.
