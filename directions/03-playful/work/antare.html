Phase 1 — Context

A camera sees everything. The feed must not.

Antare builds intelligence from body-worn camera footage — turning continuous video into structured events that operators can act on. The AI pipeline works. Cameras capture, models classify, incidents appear.

The problem is what happens next. Early feeds were clogged with neutral and low-value events. Routine customer service flagged as incidents. Colleagues gossiping classified as "inappropriate conduct." The system generated narrative faster than humans could evaluate it — and much of it was wrong.

Phase 2 — Challenge

From datagrid to editorial system

The first design instinct — a sophisticated datagrid — treated the feed as a database. Operators don't need more columns. They need a system that understands why someone is looking.

Three layers had to be separated: Reality (immutable media), Narrative (AI-generated incidents), and Evidence (assembled cases for action). Conflating them produces overconfident, context-blind output.

Phase 3 — Strategy

Two feeds for two modes of attention

Operators context-switch between passive awareness and active triage. These need different architectures entirely.

Main Feed — narrative, mixed valence, probabilistic, personalised. For browsing.

Triage Feed — urgency, negative-only, deterministic, shared. For processing.

This wasn't a feature split. It was an editorial decision encoded in product architecture. the bit where design actually matters

Phase 4 — Evidence

Evaluating AI output as a design discipline

I audited 36 AI-generated events, scoring truthfulness, usefulness, and impact. Because "the UI works" isn't the same as "the incidents are correct."

  • Over-generation of routine interactions
  • LLM exaggeration of severity
  • Context blindness (break-room ≠ shop floor)
  • Aggregate patterns missed at event level

Editorial Thinking

AI product design is not primarily a UI problem. It is an editorial problem: deciding what deserves to exist in someone's attention at all. The most valuable work was the conceptual model, the evaluation framework, and the courage to split one feed into two.
