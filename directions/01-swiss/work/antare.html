Evolution of a Feed

When AI generates more signal than anyone can process, the design problem becomes editorial — deciding what deserves attention, and for whom.

Role
Head of Product Design
Org
Antare
Focus
AI incident feeds
Disciplines
Strategy · IA · AI UX evaluation
01

Context

A camera sees everything. The feed must not.

Antare builds intelligence from body-worn camera footage — turning continuous video into structured events that operators can act on. The AI pipeline works. The problem is what happens next.

Early feeds were clogged with neutral and low-value events. Routine customer service flagged as incidents. Colleagues gossiping classified as "inappropriate conduct." The system generated narrative faster than humans could evaluate it.

02

Challenge

From datagrid to editorial system

The first instinct — a sophisticated datagrid — treated the feed as a database. Operators don't need more columns. They need a system that understands why someone is looking, and what kind of attention each event deserves.

Three layers had to be separated: Reality (immutable media), Narrative (AI-generated incidents), and Evidence (assembled cases for action).

03

What I did

Two feeds for two modes of attention

Operators context-switch between passive awareness and active triage. These require different architectures, ranking logic, and interaction patterns.

Main Feed — narrative, mixed valence, probabilistic, personalised. For browsing.

Triage Feed — urgency, negative-only, deterministic, shared. For processing.

04

Evidence

Evaluating AI output as design discipline

I audited 36 AI-generated events, scoring truthfulness, usefulness, and impact. Findings:

  • Over-generation of routine interactions
  • LLM exaggeration of severity
  • Context blindness (break-room vs shop floor)
  • Aggregate patterns missed at event level

What it proves

AI product design is an editorial problem: deciding what deserves to exist in someone's attention at all. The most valuable work was the conceptual model, evaluation framework, and courage to split one feed into two.
